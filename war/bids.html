Relooking The COE Bidding System

POSTED: 12 Sep 2013 | EDITED: 23 Sep 2013

In a press release announcing the new criteria for Category A cars, the LTA justified the current COE bidding system by citing data on the COE bidding exercises held from June to August 2013. The choice of data is strange. First, the data covers only 6 bidding exercises (out of 24 in a year) and a short time frame (3 months). Second, the data is timed after the car loan restrictions introduced by MAS in February 2013 aimed to moderate COE prices.

Nevertheless, we shall explore the data available and examine the bids in closer detail.

Among car COEs, Category B (premium cars) and Category E (open) see a wider variance of bid ranges exceeding the quota premium (QP).

What they didn’t tell you is that overbidding is common in Category D (motorcycles). A quarter of successful bids is in the >40% of QP bid range. Perhaps this is due to the lower QP of motorcycles (normally less than $2,000). The base is small so there is greater variance. Bidders could also afford to bid more.

Who Dunnit?

In a bid to reassure the public that overbidding is not a cause of the high COE prices, Transport Minister Lui Tuck Yew said that more than nine in 10 successful bids for car COEs fell within 10 percent of the closing premiums.

Among car COEs, overbidding seems to be more pronounced in Category B (premium cars) and Category E (open). Contrary to the official account, these two categories see slightly more than 10 percent of successful bids exceeding the QP by 10 percent or more. Perhaps this observation is not surprising; bidders for these two categories should have a higher propensity to spend.

Other questions remain. For the bids that exceed the QP by 10 percent or more, how many are car dealers? How different would the data be over a longer time period and/or before the car loan restrictions imposed in February 2013?

The Profit Motive

Car dealers have little incentive to overbid for COEs as it would erode their profit margins. But is this really true? At first glance, this might seem the case as dealers do provide hedging against rising COE prices in the future. Dealers offer to transact at current COE prices instead of the actual price when the COE is acquired. Should the COE price increase, the dealers make up the difference.

The current auction system makes sense when the COE price represents a true cost to every bidder. But bear in mind that dealers are agents, not principals, and their goal is profit maximising. COE prices are effectively passed on to consumers, with the hedge against rising COE prices added on as a deal sweetener. Even if COE prices should rise against the dealer’s favour and the profit margin of the current deal takes a haircut, the dealer makes up for it by closing the next deal at an even higher price.

Dealers and lenders earn commissions based on the total price of the car, which includes the COE. They have every incentive to jack up the total price of the car by overbidding on the COE and raising COE prices. This is especially true in the current climate of historically low COE quotas, which place a hard limit on the number of cars they can sell. While dealers couldn’t sell as many physical cars as before, they could manipulate COE prices to a point where the piece of paper is worth a number of times more than the car!

As Mr Tan Si An has rightly pointed out in a forum letter, “the COE system introduces a lot of distortion into the final price of a car. COE premiums can range from more than 10 times the OMV of low-end models to less than 10 per cent for a high-end vehicle.” (TODAY Voices, 20 September 2013, For COEs, best indicator of value is value itself, not proxies)

Under the new COE classification system, over 90 percent of Category A car models will have an Open Market Value (OMV) of less than $20,000. With $20,000 as the benchmark OMV of a Category A car, the corresponding COE premium from the second bidding exercise of September 2013 amounted to $83,751, which is more than 4x the value of the car. Altogether, the consumer pays for 5 cars (OMV + COE). Imagine, in a single deal, the middleman earns commission for the equivalent of 5 cars!

And so the feast continues, riding on a “famine” of COE quotas in recent years. With COE quotas in short supply, dealers help themselves to the lion’s share by crowding out individual bidders with their financial clout. Not only do dealers have more cash on hand than the typical household, they also have access to deeper credit lines from the banks. (Not to mention that banks get referral business from dealers in the form of car loans!) In this way, dealers exert considerable influence over COE prices when quotas are low.

The hefty bid deposit of $10,000 further discourages individual bidding. To put things in perspective, only 3 in 10 households boast an average monthly income exceeding $10,000.

So what can we do? Should we ban dealers from bidding outright? Should we change the COE auction system from the present Lowest-Successful-Bid (LSB) to Pay-As-You-Bid (PAYB)?

A ban on dealer bidding can be overcome by proxy. Some consumers do value the convenience of dealers bidding on their behalf. An outright ban doesn’t seem like such a good idea. It is also debatable as to whether a PAYB auction can provide greater efficiency and lower COE prices. However, the LTA can consider lowering the barriers to entry by reducing the bid deposit. This will encourage more individual bidding and reduce the risk of collusion among dealers.

As the Minister in charge of MAS Lawrence Wong had said in his parliamentary reply, high COE prices are a cause for concern as transport costs drive inflation and create negative spillover effects on the broader economy. We hope that the review of the COE system is not done yet. The system still needs change.
